1. The Need for Communication (1950s–1960s)

During the Cold War, the United States wanted a way to communicate and share information between computers, even if part of the network

So, the idea was;
  • Computers should be connected
  • Information should travel quickly
  • The network should not depend on one central point

2. 1969: Birth of ARPANET (the first real step

The US Department of Defense (through ARPA, now called DARPA) created ARPANET — the very first network using packet switching.

  • On October 29, 1969, the first message was sent between two computers: one at UCLA and one at Stanford.
  • The first message was supposed to be "LOGIN", but it crashed after just "LO" — still, it worked!

ARPANET connected only a few university and research computers at first.

3. 1970s: Email and more connections

  • 1971: First email was sent (by Ray Tomlinson).
  • Scientists kept improving the system and connecting more computers.

4. January 1, 1983: The official "birthday" of the modern internet

All networks started using the same rules called TCP/IP (Transmission Control Protocol / Internet Protocol). This allowed different networks (from military, universities, companies) to connect and "talk" to each other — this became the Internet.

5. 1989–1991: The World Wide Web (what most people use today)

The internet existed, but it was hard to use — mostly text commands, no pictures or easy clicking.

In 1989, a British scientist named Tim Berners-Lee (working at CERN in Switzerland) invented the World Wide Web:

  • Hyperlinks (clickable links)
  • Web pages written in HTML
  • URLs (web addresses like www.example.com)
  • Web browser software to view pages

He made it free for everyone in 1993.

6. 1990s: Boom for normal people

  • 1993: First easy graphical browsers (like Mosaic, then Netscape) appeared → suddenly everyone could browse pictures, videos, and websites.
  • Companies like AOL brought the internet to homes with dial-up modems.
  • By late 1990s: millions of people online, websites everywhere.
